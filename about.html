About Benjamin Ryan

The Mind Behind Keys, Codes & Modes

Benjamin Ryan has spent decades at the intersection of visual communication, technology, and music — building tools that make the invisible visible. Keys, Codes & Modes™ is the culmination of that work: a platform that transforms music theory from abstract notation into something you can actually see.

The throughline of his career is visual thinking. From pioneering computer graphics at GE Genigraphics, to crafting billion-dollar aerospace proposals at Northrop, to training FEMA emergency managers across fourteen western states — the challenge has always been the same: how do you take something complex and make it instantly understood? KCM applies that same discipline to music theory.

📚 Author & Music Educator

Author and illustrator of "Keys, Codes, and Modes: A Visual Approach to Understanding Music", Ben translates intricate music theory into a visual language that clicks — for beginners and advanced players alike. The book laid the foundation for the KCM platform's 12-Color Chromatic Spectrum™ system, which maps every note to a consistent color, making patterns in harmony and scale structure immediately recognizable rather than memorized.

🎸 Inventor & Patent Holder

As the inventor of the FreeStyle® Musical Device, Ben holds four U.S. patents for a guitar technology that fundamentally reimagines how the instrument is played. Those patents establish prior art in the field of visual music interfaces — the same conceptual territory that KCM now explores through software. With decades of musical experience across folk, rock, jazz, and country, he brings a performer's intuition to every tool he builds.

The Visual Layer Music Theory Has Been Missing

Music theory has always had a visual problem. Staff notation, interval tables, and chord charts are abstractions built for reading music — not for understanding it. For most learners, theory is a wall of symbols with no clear connection to what they hear or feel when they play.

KCM approaches music theory the way neuroscience suggests we actually learn: through pattern recognition, color association, and spatial relationships. The 12-Color Chromatic Spectrum™ gives every note a consistent color identity — yellow for C, blue for F#, and so on around the clock wheel. Scales, modes, and chord relationships become visible as geometric patterns rather than memorized lists.
